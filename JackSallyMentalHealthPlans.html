Jack & Sally: Fun & Feelings on the Board

This page is inspired by Jack Skellington and Sally from The Nightmare Before Christmas. It mixes a playful board game with a gentle mental health reflection tool.

The game follows Jack’s “mental health journey” in a fictional, symbolic way: curiosity, confusion, mistakes, and learning to listen to his heart and to others.

This is not therapy or medical advice. If you’re struggling with your mental health, please talk with a qualified professional or a trusted person in your life. You deserve real support.

How to use this page

1. Read the short “Jack’s mental health journey” cards to see how his story can mirror real feelings like restlessness, loneliness, and hope.

2. Play the Jack & Sally board game: roll the dice, move along the path, and read the small prompts on each tile.

3. Use the mental health plan helper to write your own gentle safety plan: people, places, and small actions that help you feel steadier.

Jack’s mental health journey (symbolic)

These cards are not a diagnosis. They’re just a way to describe Jack’s fictional feelings in language that can help us talk about real-life emotions.

Restless in Halloween Town

Jack feels like he’s repeating the same show every year. Everyone loves him, but he feels empty inside. This can mirror real restlessness or burnout: when life looks “fine” on the outside but feels flat on the inside.

In real life, that might be a sign to check in with yourself, slow down, or talk with someone about what you really need.

Curiosity & risky choices

Jack becomes obsessed with Christmas and rushes into big decisions without listening to warnings. Curiosity is beautiful, but risky choices can hurt him and others.

In real life, it can help to pause, ask for advice, and think about safety before jumping into something new.

Listening to Sally

Sally sees danger coming and tries to warn Jack. At first, he doesn’t listen. Later, he realizes her care and wisdom matter.

In real life, trusted friends, family, or professionals can be like Sally—people who see things we miss and want us to stay safe.

Owning mistakes & choosing care

Jack eventually admits his plan went wrong and tries to fix it. He doesn’t stay stuck in shame; he takes responsibility and chooses a kinder path.

Real mental health journeys often include mistakes, apologies, and new choices. That’s part of growth, not failure.

Jack & Sally – a tiny night scene

A small SVG illustration of Jack and Sally standing together under a Halloween moon, as a reminder that even in strange seasons, connection matters.

Jack is dramatic and searching. Sally is careful and observant. Together, they show that it’s okay to want adventure and also okay to want safety and calm.

When you think about your own mental health, you might have a “Jack” side that wants change and a “Sally” side that wants to stay safe. A good plan listens to both.

Jack-style mental health plan helper

This helper is just for reflection. It doesn’t save or send anything. You can type ideas and generate a simple “plan” to read back to yourself. For serious or ongoing struggles, please reach out to a mental health professional or a trusted person.

This is a personal reflection tool only. For intense, scary, or long-lasting feelings, please talk with a mental health professional or a trusted adult. You don’t have to carry everything alone.

Helper notes

• Jack’s side: wants change, adventure, and something new. When this side is loud, you might feel restless, bored, or frustrated.

• Sally’s side: wants safety, honesty, and calm. When this side is loud, you might feel cautious, worried, or tired.

A balanced mental health plan listens to both sides: “What do I want?” and “What keeps me safe?” Writing your plan is like letting Jack and Sally talk to each other instead of arguing.

You can print or copy your plan into a journal if you want to keep it. You can also update it as your life changes.
