Cleft Lip & Cleft Palate Surgery

A calm, plain-language guide to what these surgeries usually do, why they’re done, and how they support eating, breathing, and speech.

What is a cleft lip and cleft palate?

A cleft lip is a gap or opening in the upper lip. A cleft palate is an opening in the roof of the mouth. These happen before birth, while the baby is still developing.

Surgery does not “erase” the story of the child—it gently brings tissues together so the lip and mouth can work better for feeding, breathing, and speaking.

How surgery usually helps

Surgeons carefully move and stitch the tissues so the lip and/or palate can close. This creates a more complete barrier between the mouth and nose, which helps:

  • Keep food and drink from leaking into the nose
  • Support clearer speech sounds over time
  • Guide teeth and jaw growth more safely
Typical lip repair Around 3–6 months of age, depending on the child and team.
Typical palate repair Often in the first year or so, to support early speech and feeding.
Later care Some children need more surgeries, orthodontics, or speech therapy as they grow.
