👀 Reading Comprehension — Understanding a Text

Core idea: English II expects you to explain what a text means and how the author creates meaning.

  • Main Idea: What the text is mostly about.
  • Supporting Details: Facts, examples, or quotes that prove the main idea.
  • Author’s Purpose: To inform, persuade, entertain, or express feelings/ideas.
  • Tone: The author’s attitude (hopeful, serious, joyful, angry, calm, etc.).
  • Theme: The big message about life or human experience.

How to answer reading questions:

  • Use complete sentences.
  • Include evidence (a quote or detail) from the text.
  • Explain why that evidence matters.
Sentence frame:
The author shows that ________. This is supported by the line, “________,” which suggests ________.
✨ Literary Devices — Tools Authors Use

These are like the HTML/CSS/JavaScript of English class — the building blocks of style and meaning.

  • Simile: Comparison using like or as.
  • Metaphor: Direct comparison without using like or as.
  • Imagery: Language that appeals to the senses (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell).
  • Symbolism: An object that represents a deeper idea.
  • Foreshadowing: Hints about what will happen later.
  • Irony: The opposite of what is expected.
  • Theme: The message about life or human nature.
Symbolism examples (storybook style):
A daisy might symbolize innocence or a new beginning.
A white rose might symbolize peace, forgiveness, or purity.
A tulip might symbolize hope, renewal, or emotional growth.
✏️ Writing Skills — Strong Paragraphs (T.E.X.A.S.)

A complete English II paragraph often follows the T.E.X.A.S. structure:

T — Topic The main idea of the paragraph.
E — Evidence A quote or detail from the text.
X — eXplanation Explain what the evidence means.
A — Analysis Connect to theme, symbolism, or deeper meaning.
S — Summary Wrap up the idea and connect back to the topic.
Example paragraph

In the story, the author shows that courage often begins quietly. The narrator explains, “She stepped forward even though her voice trembled.” This detail reveals that bravery is not the absence of fear but the decision to move anyway. The moment symbolizes a turning point where the character chooses growth over comfort. Overall, the author uses this scene to teach that courage can start small but still change everything.

🧩 Essay Writing — Full Structure

A standard English II essay usually includes an introduction, body paragraphs, and a conclusion.

Introduction
  • Hook (interesting opening).
  • Background or context.
  • Thesis statement (your main argument).
Body Paragraphs
  • Use T.E.X.A.S. structure.
  • Include transitions between ideas.
  • Focus on analysis, not just summary.
Conclusion
  • Restate the thesis in new words.
  • Summarize your main points.
  • End with a final insight or connection.
Thesis frame:
The author uses ________, ________, and ________ to show that ________.

Subject–verb agreement

Transitions (however, therefore, meanwhile, in addition)
Run‑on (incorrect)
She loved to read she visited the library often.
Correct sentence
She loved to read, so she visited the library often.
🎨 Creative Writing Practice

English II also includes narrative (story) writing. Strong narratives usually have:

  • Clear setting (where and when).
  • Developed characters.
  • Conflict (a problem or challenge).
  • Rising action (events building up).
  • Climax (the most intense moment).
  • Resolution (how it ends).
  • Theme (what the story is really about).
Mini‑prompt Write a short scene where a flower (daisy, white rose, or tulip) becomes a symbol for a character’s emotional change.
✅ Study Guide Summary
  • Identify theme, tone, purpose, and main idea.
  • Use evidence and analysis in your answers.
  • Write clear paragraphs with structure (T.E.X.A.S.).
  • Recognize literary devices and explain their effect.
  • Edit for grammar, punctuation, and clarity.
  • Build essays with intro, body, and conclusion.
  • Practice creative storytelling with symbolism and emotion.
